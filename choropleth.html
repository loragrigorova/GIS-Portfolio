SDG 5 Choropleth Map

An interactive map showcasing the percentage of parliamentary seats held by women in each country, in accordance with SDG target 5.5.1.

Overview

This map depicts one of the target indicators of the United Nations' fifth Sustainable Development Goal: Achieve gender equality and empower all women and girls. More specifically, More specifically, the indicator I have depicted here is 5.5.1 (a): Proportion of seats held by women in national parliaments (United Nations Statistics Division, 2026). Through this indicator, we can measure the extent to which each country is working towards achieving Target 5.5, which aims to ensure women’s full and effective participation and equal opportunities for leadership at all levels of decision-making. The percentage of women’s representation in national parliaments is a useful metric for measuring progress toward Target 5.5, as it provides a direct and quantifiable overview of the degree to which women participate in and influence political leadership and decision-making. In order to understand the message of this map, we must first understand what a choropleth map is. A choropleth map is a type of map that assigns specific colours to geographic areas (countries, states, regions, etc) based on a corresponding data value. The difference of the data value is usually conveyed through varying the colour shade or intensity.

Methods

Both the static and interactive version of this map were made with QGIS, with the addition of Leaflet for the interactive map. I have used the most recently available data as of the time of making this, namely the proportion of seats held by women in national parliaments in 2026. This data was accessed through the database of the United Nations Statistics Division and mapped in QGIS using a standardised map of the world, also provivded by the UN. After joining the datasets in the attribute tables, I chose to use a slightly modified version of the Blue-Purple gradient colour scheme provided by ColorBrewer. I modified it by making the two colours that depict the first two lowest data classes tint more towards blue. I felt that the initial very pale blue of the lowest class in the original gradient scheme may have been too easy to confuse with countries that lack data, which are depicted in a light grey shade. Afterwards, I made sure to visually highlight the countries that have 50% or more of seats in parliament occupied by women.

Results

According to the 2026 data, seven countries in the world have more than 50% of seats in parliament held by women: Rwanda, Cuba, Nicaragua, Bolivia, Mexic, the United Arab Emirates and Andorra.

Reflection

A choropleth map, like any other, has both its benefits and drawbacks. It can be very useful for depicting large scale patterns, as it helps compare at a glance the number of parliament seats occupied by women across regions of the world. Thus, we can easily identify general trends in different parts of the world and highlight the countries with highest and lowest representation quickly. However, this is a double-edged sword: the grouping of countries into only five (in this case) colour categories can obscure the differences between countries that fall into the same data class. In other words, the same technique that lets us depict large scale patterns can oversimplify the data.

The creation of this map was my introduction to the basics of working with QGIS: I learned how to import data from external sources (in this case, the United Nation Statistics Division), change the projection of a map, execute tabular joins, classify statistical data. I also learnt a lot about the precision of cartography, something which I, as a layperson, had not really taken into consideration before: the choice of which data ranges to depict, which colours to associate with them and ensuring that the sum of all these parts remain visually harmonious took me a while, as I realised the extent to which every detail can affect the way the map is interpreted.
