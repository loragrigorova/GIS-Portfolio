Land Classification

Three maps depicting how the landscape of the river Tongelreep has changed over time.

Overview

For centuries, cartographers had to observe and manually record each feature of a landscape by going there in person and visiting every single body of water, farm field, factory and so on. Automated land cover classification seeks to make this process faster and more efficient by using satellite imagery and algorithms that allow large areas to be sorted into land-cover categories. As each surface captured by a satellite has its own unique multispectral data, or in simpler words, reflects different wavelengths, that data can be used to classify each surface by land-cover or use. The applications of this method are myriad: with it, we can study changes in landscape over time, the health of vegetation, deforestation, the effect of different agricultural methods, urban development and other changes in an environment. Supervised classification needs training samples of confirmed land-cover types, which the software uses as examples to classify the rest of the image by “inferring”. Unsupervised classification, on the other hand, has the software automatically group pixels or objects (polygons) based on similar spectral values that it detects. The only human input here is the user deciding the number of classes for the data to be divided into. Afterwards, it is up to the user to interpret and label each category and analyse whether it actually reflects reality.

Methods

These maps were produced first by choosing a 19th century map from the OldMapsOnline database and narrowing in on the area which I wanted to explore further, namely the Tongelreep. Having picked out an area of interest, I then located the very same area in present day in QGIS using OpenStreetMap. Afterwards, I downloaded the corresponding DTM and DSM files, which presented a fascinating almost-“cross-cut” look into the area. Now that I had all necessary components of my model, I overlaid them as different layers to reveal the change the area had underwent in the past (roughly) 200 years. In total, I created three maps: a digital terrain model, a digital surface model and a digital terrain model overlaid with the 19th century map, allowing us to trace if and how the river has diverged and whether surrounding areas have also undergone significant changes. The total size of the area I inspected was roughly between 2 and 5 km.

Results

When overlaid with the 1841 map, we can see that the river has diverged somewhat in the present day, although not to an extent that would significantly change the landscape. Interestingly, I found more divergence within the northern part of the river, whereas I expected more to be found further down south, due to the canals made by the Achel Abbey monks. The map cuts off almost exactly where the fishing ponds were created, an area which would have been very interesting to compare. It would also be interesting to explore how the river has changed even further downwards, in the Achel area, if such data exists.
